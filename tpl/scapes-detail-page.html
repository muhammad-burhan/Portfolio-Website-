Design Challenge

Design an augmented reality application that mitigates chronic loneliness by developing community, authentic relationships, and self-expression using AR & Art.

Solution

Scapes is an experience that facilitate users to express themselves in a safe environment through Augmented reality and Art. Using collaboration and sharing features users will be able to develop authentic connections and sense of community.

Contribution

Directed team of five to bring consistency in design. Participated in Qualitative Research, Ideation, Information Architecture, Wireframes, Visual effects and solely designed & developed product website from concept to launch.


Research

At the start of the project, team set the research goals and divided into sub-teams. After every research activity, sub-teams discussed their findings with the whole team to start making connections.

Research Goals

  • Find the who, what, where, when, and why of loneliness.
  • Know what tools & methods are successful in mitigating loneliness.
  • Learn how people develop communities founded on authentic relationships.
  • Learn how art helps people express themselves.

Total Data Points

Preliminary Survey

We sent out a preliminary survey to gain insight at a larger scale into how often our target users feel lonely and what types of situations perpetuate this feeling.

Most of the survey responses were ambiguous and this gave us insight on the preferred terminology used by our audience, and helped us avoid confuse wording of questions during intercepts and interviews.

Intercepts

We conducted 60 intercepts across Savannah to understand the public's current mental model towards art as a medium for self-expression and how their intimate social circles developed.

Initially participants were hesitant in answering questions related to loneliness because of the stigma attached to it. But later after some warm-up questions participants were able to open-up. We used these findings and data to scope our interview questions moving forward.

Interviews

Staging 30 min user meet-ups allowed for us to uncover a more in-depth understanding of how each beat of the prompt related to the users’ daily lives.

“Recently I've stopped posting a lot because I’d rather do it face to face when there is a certain level of friendship versus texting or online. If they want to know how I feel online, they’ll ask, so I don't feel the need to tell the world how I'm is doing.” - Participant

“A sense of vulnerability comes with art - painting or drawing, it gives you a sense of vulnerability because of how they respond to the piece.” - Participant

“I know I need to reach out first, but I get intimidated because I don't want to feel rejected.” - Participant

We noticed a commonality that many participants measured a relationship's authenticity by the amount of effort put forth. Yet, many participants expressed how they feared reaching out first due to the risk of rejection.

Cultural Probes

We put out cultural probes posting the questions of what it means to be a part of a community and what feelings were associated with it in relation to how often participants preferred to be alone.

“I need friends around me so I don't fully realize how alone we really are.” - Participant

We got mixed responses from cultural probes. People had both good and bad experiences in communities and some participants even related community to anxiety and depression.

Expert Interviews

We sought out six experts’ opinions on how to build a community founded on strong authentic relationships. These experts varied from community specialists such as resident directors and the director of counseling services at SCAD, to behavioral experts such as a neurologist and psychoanalyst.

“It is really important to have a common goal or purpose within a group of people. Everyone should agree on this purpose.” - Melanie Miller, Psychoanalyst

“Authentic relationships have certain amount of depth, certain amount of vulnerability. Person is willing to do or share a personal piece with other people.” - Christopher Corbett, Psychologist

“We have to make sure we are not forcing choices but providing opportunities and let them pick. They still might feel little vulnerable but they going to at least feel like they are in control.” - Christopher Corbett, Psychologist

“The trick is, how do you help someone get to a point of being vulnerable may be without them realizing it. That's where structure or technology or activities are going to help.” - Christopher Corbett, Psychologist

“People who are lonely are going to seek others who make them feel more alive but you can't find happiness solely in other people” - Allison Shapiro-Davis, Psychologist

“Loneliness is a consequence of lack of empathy, lack of desire and fear of rejection.” - Gley de Menezes, Psychoanalyst

We Learned, our society moves so fast nowadays that relationships have difficulty going past the superficial stage. For meaningful connections, people need to slow down, which is paradoxical: what we need to do is to do less, which is hard in a society that is constantly telling us to do more and faster. Additionally, different people suffers from varying degree of loneliness which affects their behavior.

Pop-up Art Study

We set up a drawing probe in Forsyth Park and encouraged people to express why they came to the park that day. We chose this prompt because sharing a common interest is the first step in building a relationship. We learned, adding onto previous creations lowers the entry barrier for those who lacked artistic background.

Competitive Analysis

We examined the competitive applications, programs, and events which attempted to draw people together, champion art, or utilize augmented reality. Many augmented reality applications restricted their tools for self-expression to a limited set of colors, amount of text and choice of emoticons. We also found that the augmented reality applications in the market lack the opportunity for users to collaborate on works of art, and simply focus on an individual’s personal experience.

Research Analysis

Archetypes

Our research insights led us creating three unique archetypes. Using this, we could create scenarios based on our research, and pinpoint moments in their daily lives in which our app could enhance their experiences and ease their pains.

Lone Wolf

Lone Wolves see society as hostile and shelter themselves from the world due to their fear of being rejected.

Wall Flower

Wall flowers can feel loneliness even in the company of others and often feel overlooked or unimportant. They feel a lack of connection to people around them.

Social Butterfly

Social Butterflies have no problem meeting new people, but can still feel lonely due to a lack of deeper connection or intimacy others.

User Needs, Likes/Dislikes and Motivations

By determining users' needs, behaviors, likes and dislikes, we prioritized certain needs that we would focus on. These needs were divided and a affinitized to discover similar patterns.

Design Principles

Analyzing user needs, likes/dislikes, behavior & motivation we formulated our design principles, which helped us know exactly which user we were designing for.

Design Principle 01

Facilitate collaborative creation and consumption of art.

Design Principle 02

Ensuring a safe place for self-expression.

Design Principle 03

Provide actions and activities that are passively available.

Design Principle 04

Make users feel recognized and appreciated.

Model for Mitigating Loneliness

Our design principles were the culmination of our research. The needs and likes/dislikes of our archetypes (that were created based on our interviews), were complemented and checked against the information of our experts and directed towards areas our competitors were lacking in. These served as our guiding compass when creating our initial wave of concepts.

This model is not suggesting that a Lone Wolf will become a Social Butterfly, rather we identified how each might begin to form an authentic relationship. Through our research we learned that if a person is suffering from Chronic Loneliness an App might not help him, it is recommended for him to see the consultant for that. Moving forward, we decided to focus more center three steps.

Ideation

Early Concepts

Based on design principles and user need, we came up with six concepts. After getting feedback from Google Daydream team and analyzing the technological limitations, we decided to proceed with EastAR egg and Geo Grams.

ARtist Lenses

This concept allows users to transform their worlds into master paintings. When users scan art, they unlock other artists’ lenses. Users can also share their lenses with friends.

Event Discovery

Local businesses or individuals can craft experiences for users to partake in. Users will be recommended events based on their interests and can notify friends if they are going. AR bread crumbs will lead to the event location. These trails will be tailored to fit the theme of the event.

Bat Signal

By allowing users to create their own visual flag, they can alert their friends of potential interests without fearing direct rejection that is associated with an immediate question.

Ultimate Companion

This AR pal can generate topics based on mutual interests between its human and other nearby users. It can also initiate the first conversation, alleviating any stress that comes from making the first move.

Geo-Gram

Users are encouraged to share their messages and illustrations with their friends. They can easily show their feelings through personalized messages pinned to a specific geo location. Others can then respond or continue to expand on other messages.

East-AR Eggs

Users can leave AR art installations in specific locations of interest for friends to discover. Those who discover the installations are prompted to replace it with their own art or add to it. This includes a history of the past concepts. These installations can be edited by anyone who finds them.

User Tasks

Moving with EastAR egg and Geo Grams as our base concepts we start defining user tasks. We also incorporated different ideas from other concepts to target our three archetypes but also made sure we are always referring our design principles to validate our ideas.

Users Should be able...
  • to create different models using primitive shapes.
  • to edit their creations using some basic tools (Scale, rotate, move, duplicate).
  • to attach sound and animate objects.
  • to save, send or place their creations anywhere on the map.
  • to discover user created content on the map.
  • to get notified when they are nearby creation which they haven't discovered.
  • to add on the top of the public creations and get notified when other people are adding on theirs's.
  • to see the timeline of creation representing how creation has changed over time and who has contributed on this creation.
  • to collaborate on their creations live with others.
  • to go live while they are creating alone or in collaboration.
  • to watch broadcasted live streams of other users while they are working on their creations.
  • to report inappropriate content.

Initial Prototypes & Testing

After defining the sketching out the basic wireframes and we also start testing how user will use different tools to create objects in the Augmented reality. We tested different prototypes to answer how can we place toolbox in Augmented reality and how user can interact with tools to build his/her creations.

Another Iteration

From user testing we learned users were not at all comfortable with moving toolbox. We observed users were getting more comfortable in using the Augmented reality toolbelt instead of tool boxes. Moreover, the app flow was really confusing to users. Partially because of the low fidelity of it. Other than that, they were not able to make sense of collaboration on the project and how they are sending their creations to people on the map. After identifying these problems we came up with another set of prototypes and tested it with the users.

Wireframes 2.0

In next iteration we detach the collaboration feature from the canvas placement and put the top center of the screen which brings the list of people to collaborate. This facilitate user to invite other people later and start the creation without making a choice. We also combined creation placement on map and sending to a friend on one screen to avoid giving the user better context of what they are supposed to do after working on their creation.

Getting feedback! Quickly!

In parallel, we start doing testing intercepts around the campus to quickly validate our prototypes and get user's feedback.

Hi-fi Prototypes & Testing


After couple of more iterations we made a hi-fidelity prototypes to get more granular feedback about the specific interactions like how you will move the object, scale it and animate it. Animating an object was another challenge. We tested the prototype in which you will move your phone in exactly the same path you want your object to move. And users loved it!

During the implementation phase we never forgot our design principles and was continuously validating our ultimate goal of mitigating the disconnection between users using art!

Final Solution

Home Screen

The landing page of Scapes serves as a viewfinder into the augmented world by using the phone’s camera with a minimalistic, material design UI. At your fingertips, you can access the discovery map, collaborate with your friends see your profile or start creating art.

Creation Tool

The core of Scapes presents you with a toolbelt that follows you around and can be seen with a small down tilt of your phone, providing easy access while not obstructing your view. You can use it to select primitive shapes, base models organized in categories, particle effects, saved assets or access Google’s Low Poly library for more.

Editing Tool

With one or multiple objects selected, the user can pinch to scale them, hold the finger and move around to rotate, or select one of the various functionalities on the sidebar. Those include moving the object, grouping or un-grouping them, duplicating it, deleting it, changing its color, attaching sound, adding gravity or creating a motion path for it to follow.

Collaboration

User can host a collaborative art session and invite friends or join existing ones that are being hosted by their friends or in public mode. The users’ avatars serve as a stand-in if they are not in the same location and highlights indicate the sections they are working on.

Going Live

While creating your art, be it solo or collaboratively, you can start live-streaming at any moment for people around the world to view and react to it. You can get notified if people live-stream works using your art or if your friend starts one, or you can simply search for them using certain filters.

Share Map

After you are done creating your art, you can use the share map to place it anywhere in the world or, conversely, let it sit exactly where you created it for context-dependent art. When placing your art, you can make it public, friends-only or leave it for a specific friend to find, as well as change the settings whether people can save it as an asset or add to it.

Discovery Map

From the home screen user can access the discovery map where they can view live streams, join open collaboration and look for creations around them. Undiscovered creations are represented by the abstract icons and get replaced by the actual creation once you find it in physical space.

Discovering Creations

Either by notification, tracking through the map or randomly walking up on it, your home screen is your viewfinder into the augmented world of scapes, and lets you find creations others have placed in the world. You can also add onto creations and it will generate a timeline that users can scroll through to see how the art has evolved overtime.

Adding onto Creations

After discovering the creations, you can add onto it by jumping directly into the creation tool. Moreover, you can save that creation to your tool belt for future use.

Website goal

Design and develop a website that can serve as a documentation for the Google Daydream team. Moreover, website should also give the brief introduction of the Scapes and should also cover the brief design process of the team.

Initial Layouts

Initially I was thinking of dividing the website into two main sections, the product itself and our team story containing multiple videos. But later considering our process and the amount of information I had to display I changed the layout to four pages.

  • Home: The basic intro of the app and the basic models.
  • Scapes: Detailed explanation of the Google Scapes.
  • Process: Goals, Implementation and insights from each process.
  • Team: Brief introduction of the individuals in team and their contact information.
Sitemap
Full Page Layout

On the home page, the challenge was to give the brief introduction and also displaying the foundation of Scapes like who the users are, what are the design principles and how it's expected to build authentic relationships. All of this information was causing a long scrolling and the information had to be displayed in the chronological order to make sure viewer knows how we came up with design principles and same was the case with most of the pages. So, I decided to go with the section scroll layout and within each section I used carousals to encapsulate the content. This end up giving two benefits.
1) User can now focus on the one piece of information at a time.
2) The information is not overwhelming, if user wants to know more he/she can use carousal to get more information with in sections.

Development

The website was developed using basic front-end technologies like HTML5, SASS, Flexbox, JavaScript and JQuery. The website is not using any boilerplate or template and everything is written from scratch expect supporting plugins like fullPage.js, owl.carousal etc. This enables developer to have the full control over the layout and styling.
